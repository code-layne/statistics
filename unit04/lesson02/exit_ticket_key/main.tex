\begin{scenariobox}[Context]{sky}
\small
A bag contains 3 red marbles and 7 blue marbles. A student draws one marble,
records the color, and \textbf{replaces} it. She repeats this process 10 times
and draws red on 4 of the 10 trials.
\end{scenariobox}

\vspace{0.1in}

\begin{practicebox}
\small
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=8pt]
  \item \ans{$\hat{p} = 4/10 = 0.40$. The empirical probability of drawing red is 0.40.}

  \item \ans{$P(\text{red}) = 3/10 = 0.30$. There are 3 red marbles out of 10 total.}

  \item \ans{\ldots as the number of trials increases, the empirical (simulated) probability tends to get closer to the true probability of the event.}

  \item \ans{Yes. With only 10 trials there is considerable random variation, so getting 4 red (0.40) instead of the expected 3 (0.30) is common. With 100 trials, the Law of Large Numbers says the relative frequency will stabilize closer to the true value of 0.30 because random fluctuations average out over more trials.}
\end{enumerate}
\end{practicebox}
